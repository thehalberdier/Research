GPU Primer: From Zero to 80%

Author

Research Notes

Published

March 17, 2026

Goal: Get you to ~80% of GPUs, fast. No prior hardware knowledge assumed. Covers: GPU fundamentals, data center vs consumer GPUs, key specs, major chips (H200, B200, MI325X), how AI uses GPUs, and a consumer buying guide.

1. What Even Is a GPU?

A CPU (your laptop’s main chip) is like a brilliant generalist — it handles a few complex tasks at a time, really fast.

A GPU (Graphics Processing Unit) is like an army of interns — thousands of simpler cores doing millions of small tasks simultaneously. Originally designed to render pixels on a screen, it turns out this “do everything in parallel” design is perfect for:

  • Training AI models (billions of matrix multiplications)
  • Running AI inference (serving ChatGPT responses)
  • Scientific simulations
  • Cryptocurrency mining
  • 3D graphics and gaming

The GPU revolution in AI happened because matrix math — the core of neural networks — maps perfectly onto what GPUs are built to do.

2. The Two Worlds of GPUs

There are two very different GPU markets, and they’re easy to confuse:

🎮 Consumer / Gaming GPUs

  • Brands: NVIDIA GeForce (RTX 4090, RTX 5090), AMD Radeon (RX 9070)
  • Goal: Render beautiful graphics at high frame rates
  • Price: ₹30,000 – ₹2,00,000
  • VRAM: 8GB – 24GB

🧠 Data Center / AI GPUs

  • Brands: NVIDIA H100, H200, B200 / AMD Instinct MI300X, MI325X
  • Goal: Train and run large AI models (LLMs, image models)
  • Price: $20,000 – $100,000+ per card
  • VRAM: 80GB – 192GB
  • These are what power ChatGPT, Gemini, Claude

Unless you’re building a supercomputer, consumer GPUs are what you’d buy. But understanding data center GPUs helps you understand what’s powering the AI tools you use every day.

3. Key Specs — What Actually Matters

🧠 VRAM (Video RAM)

The GPU’s own memory. The most critical spec for AI work.

  • More VRAM = bigger models you can run or train
  • A 7B parameter model needs ~14GB VRAM minimum
  • A 70B model needs ~140GB — which is why you need multiple GPUs or specialized chips

Think of VRAM like your workbench. If the model doesn’t fit on the bench, you can’t work on it.

⚡ Memory Bandwidth

How fast data can move between VRAM and the compute cores. Measured in GB/s or TB/s.

  • More bandwidth = faster training and inference
  • Often more important than raw compute power for AI workloads
  • The H200 has ~3.35 TB/s — insane for its time

🔢 TFLOPS (Tera Floating Point Operations Per Second)

Raw compute power. Higher = more math per second.

  • Different precisions matter: FP64 (scientific), FP32 (standard), FP16/BF16 (AI training), INT8/FP8 (AI inference)
  • AI workloads primarily use FP16/BF16 or FP8
  • The H100 does ~3,958 TFLOPS at FP8

🔌 TDP (Thermal Design Power)

How much electricity the GPU uses (and heat it generates). In watts.

  • Consumer GPU: 150–450W
  • Data center GPU: 300–1,000W
  • Running 8x B200 GPUs = ~8,000W. That’s why AI costs so much to run.

🔗 Interconnects (NVLink, Infinity Fabric)

How multiple GPUs talk to each other.

  • Training large models requires many GPUs working together
  • NVLink (NVIDIA): ultra-fast GPU-to-GPU connection, up to 1.8 TB/s between cards
  • Infinity Fabric (AMD): AMD’s equivalent
  • Slow interconnects are a bottleneck — like having brilliant workers but a slow network

4. The Major Players

🟢 NVIDIA — The Dominant Force

NVIDIA owns ~80%+ of the AI GPU market. Here’s why:

  • First mover in GPU compute (CUDA platform, 2006)
  • CUDA is the programming layer that software is written for. Almost all AI frameworks (PyTorch, TensorFlow) are deeply optimized for CUDA.
  • This software ecosystem moat is enormous — AMD hardware is competitive, but software support still lags

NVIDIA GPU Families:

Family Era Use Case
GeForce RTX (3000s, 4000s, 5000s) Consumer Gaming, hobbyist AI
V100 2017 First major AI GPU
A100 2020 Trained GPT-3 era models
H100 2022 ChatGPT era, still widely used
H200 2024 H100 + HBM3e memory upgrade
B100 / B200 2024–25 Blackwell architecture, next-gen
GB200 2025 CPU+GPU combo, for AI clusters

🔴 AMD — The Challenger

  • Consumer: AMD Radeon competes well with NVIDIA GeForce on price/performance
  • Data center: AMD Instinct MI-series (MI250, MI300X, MI325X, MI350X)
  • AMD’s big advantage: higher VRAM on some chips (MI300X has 192GB)
  • Big disadvantage: ROCm (AMD’s CUDA equivalent) is less mature; software support still catching up
  • Companies like Meta and Microsoft are actively investing in AMD to reduce NVIDIA dependence

🔵 Intel — The Newcomer

  • Intel Arc: Consumer gaming GPUs (competitive at budget tiers)
  • Gaudi 3: Data center AI GPU — competitive specs on paper, ecosystem is nascent
  • Not a major player in AI yet but investing heavily

🇨🇳 Huawei Ascend — The Wildcard

  • China’s answer to NVIDIA, accelerated by US export controls
  • Ascend 910B/910C — competitive with H100 on some benchmarks
  • Mostly used within China’s AI ecosystem

5. The Big Chips Explained

NVIDIA H200

  • Released: Early 2024
  • Architecture: Hopper (same as H100)
  • The upgrade: Switched from HBM2e to HBM3e memory — 141GB VRAM (up from 80GB) and 3.35 TB/s bandwidth
  • FP8 compute: ~3,958 TFLOPS
  • TDP: 700W
  • The H200 is essentially an H100 with a memory upgrade. The real architectural leap was H100 → B200 (Blackwell).
  • Who uses it: Every major cloud provider (AWS, Azure, Google Cloud), AI labs

NVIDIA B200 (Blackwell)

  • Released: 2024–2025
  • Architecture: Blackwell — NVIDIA’s next major architecture after Hopper
  • VRAM: 192GB HBM3e
  • Bandwidth: 8 TB/s
  • FP4 compute: ~18,000 TFLOPS (new FP4 precision for inference)
  • TDP: ~1,000W
  • Key innovation: NVLink Switch — connects up to 576 GPUs as one giant brain
  • The GB200 is a superchip: a Grace CPU + 2x B200 GPUs in one package, designed for AI servers (NVL72 racks)
  • Who uses it: Microsoft, Google, Amazon, Meta — spending billions on Blackwell clusters

AMD MI325X

  • Released: Late 2024
  • Architecture: CDNA 3 (same as MI300X, with memory upgrade)
  • VRAM: 256GB HBM3 — the most of any GPU available
  • Bandwidth: 6 TB/s
  • FP8 compute: ~2,600 TFLOPS
  • TDP: 750W
  • Strength: Massive VRAM means it can fit the biggest models on a single chip without multi-GPU tricks
  • Who uses it: Microsoft Azure, Oracle, some Meta deployments

6. How AI Models Use GPUs

Training vs. Inference

Training = Teaching the model (happens once, very expensive)

  • Requires thousands of GPUs for months
  • GPT-4 reportedly cost $100M+ to train
  • Needs massive VRAM and compute

Inference = Using the model to answer your questions (happens billions of times)

  • Can use fewer/cheaper GPUs
  • Latency matters more than raw throughput
  • Optimized with quantization (FP8, INT4) to fit bigger models on smaller hardware

Why VRAM is Everything for AI

When you run a model, the entire model must fit in VRAM:

  • LLaMA 3 8B: ~16GB VRAM (FP16)
  • LLaMA 3 70B: ~140GB VRAM (FP16), or ~35GB with aggressive quantization
  • GPT-4 class (~1T parameters): needs hundreds of GPUs

This is why data center GPUs have 80–256GB VRAM while your gaming GPU has 8–24GB.

Model Parallelism

When a model is too big for one GPU, you split it:

  • Tensor Parallelism: Split individual layers across GPUs
  • Pipeline Parallelism: Different GPUs handle different layers
  • Data Parallelism: Same model on multiple GPUs, each processing different data batches

This is why fast interconnects (NVLink) matter so much — GPUs are constantly talking to each other.

7. The GPU Shortage & Economics

Why GPUs Are So Expensive and Hard to Get

  1. Demand explosion: ChatGPT’s launch triggered a GPU gold rush
  2. Supply constraints: GPUs are made on cutting-edge nodes (TSMC’s 4nm/3nm). Capacity is limited.
  3. Export controls: US restricted NVIDIA GPU exports to China (China gets limited H20 chips instead of full H100s)
  4. Long lead times: Major cloud orders take 6–12 months to fulfil

The Power Problem

A single rack of 8x H100s uses ~40,000 watts. A data center with thousands of GPUs consumes as much electricity as a small city. This is why:

  • AI companies are building their own power plants
  • Nuclear energy is getting renewed interest (Microsoft, Google)
  • “AI electricity cost” is now a major factor in model pricing

If You Want to Run AI Locally

  • Minimum: 16GB VRAM (run 7B–13B models)
  • Sweet spot: 24GB VRAM (run up to 30B models with quantization)
  • Serious: 2x 24GB GPUs (64B+ models)

9. Key Terms Glossary

Term Meaning
VRAM GPU’s own memory — bigger = better for AI
HBM High Bandwidth Memory — stacked memory chips used in data center GPUs (HBM2e → HBM3 → HBM3e)
GDDR7 Consumer GPU memory (like RAM but faster)
CUDA NVIDIA’s GPU programming platform — the reason NVIDIA dominates AI
ROCm AMD’s equivalent to CUDA
Tensor Cores Specialized cores in NVIDIA GPUs for matrix math (AI)
TFLOPS Trillion floating point operations per second — raw compute power
FP32/FP16/FP8/INT4 Number precisions — lower = faster and uses less memory, slightly less accurate
NVLink NVIDIA’s fast GPU-to-GPU interconnect
PCIe Standard interface connecting GPU to motherboard (slower than NVLink)
TDP Total power draw in watts
Blackwell NVIDIA’s current architecture (B100, B200, GB200)
Hopper NVIDIA’s previous architecture (H100, H200)
CDNA AMD’s data center GPU architecture
RDNA AMD’s consumer/gaming GPU architecture
SXM NVIDIA’s high-performance server GPU form factor (vs PCIe)
